## Synopsis

Almost every chapter from here onward uses interrupts in some form: the CTC tick, the line interrupt that drives mid-frame palette tricks, the DMA-end signal, the UART receive interrupt, even the Copper. Rather than re-explaining the mechanics each time, we pay the conceptual cost once — right here — and let later chapters say *"enable the line interrupt as in [Interrupts](./04-interrupts.mdx)"* in a single sentence.

This chapter covers:

- **The Z80 interrupt modes.** IM0 (legacy CP/M), IM1 (the Spectrum default — `RST $38`), and **IM2** (vectored, the one that matters on the Next).
- **The I and R registers.** What they do, why `LD I,A` plus `IM 2` is the standard incantation, and the alignment trick that lets you use a single all-`$FF`-byte vector table.
- **Maskable vs. non-maskable.** When the NMI fires (the Multiface/divMMC button on real hardware; Klive can simulate the press), and why most code never touches it.
- **The Next's hardware IM2 controller.** NextRegs `$C0`–`$C8`: configurable vector top bits, per-source enables, per-source status. One IM2 vector per source, computed automatically from a fixed priority number — no daisy chain, no hand-rolled vector dispatch.
- **The interrupt sources.** ULA frame, line interrupt, all four CTC channels, UART RX, UART TX, DMA-end, joystick I/O, mouse, divMMC, expansion bus.
- **Writing a clean ISR.** Register save/restore, when to `EI` before `RETI`, latency budgeting, and the "store everything in shadow registers" pattern.
